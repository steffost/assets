Syreplantage

In the heart of The Pearl, three levels below the residential ring where colonists sleep and children learn to read, the air tastes different—cleaner, somehow more alive, carrying a faint mineral sweetness that lingers on the tongue like the aftermath of rain. This is Syreplantage, the oxygen farm that breathes for everyone aboard Ombra Prime. The room occupies a vast cylindrical space carved from the ship's infrastructure, a cathedral of glass and living light where the boundary between machine and organism has long since dissolved into something approaching symbiosis.

The space rises three stories, its ceiling a curved smart glass biodome that shimmers with whatever time of day the colony's circadian programming dictates. In the artificial dawn, golden light spills down through the dome like honey poured slow, warming the tops of the bioreactors and coaxing the algae within to begin their work. By midday, the light intensifies to a bright white, supplemented by rows of full-spectrum LEDs in rich reds and deep blues—wavelengths that the microalgae consume with particular greed, converting photons into oxygen with an efficiency that still amazes the xenobiologists. At dusk, everything shifts to amber and rose, and finally at night, the overhead illumination dims to almost nothing, leaving the room lit only by the algae themselves: a soft, pulsing blue-green bioluminescence that seems to breathe with the entire space, rising and falling in slow rhythms that no algorithm fully controls.

Syreplantage houses forty-seven towering bioreactors, each a cylinder of ultra-clear bioplastic standing nearly nine meters tall and two meters in diameter. They cluster in concentric rings around a central monitoring alcove, their surfaces curved like pillars of trapped ocean. Within them, the world is different—thick, soupy, alive with billions upon billions of microorganisms. Chlorella strains in one bank, their tiny green spheres stacking into clouds that scatter light into emerald haze. Spirulina in another, their blue-green spirals twisting like tiny galaxies. And in the deepest tanks, engineered extremophiles from the Mariana Trench and the ice-covered lakes of Europa—organisms that have never known gentle conditions, adapted now to thrive in this carefully maintained chaos.

The air exchange system hums constantly, drawing carbon dioxide-rich atmosphere from the animal habitats and aquaculture bays through microbial scrubbers before feeding the cleaned, concentrated gas into the bioreactors. The algae drink the CO2 and exhale pure oxygen, which flows upward through the space, carrying the faint sweetness of their respiration into the colony's lungs. Everything here is measured, monitored, balanced: inline turbidity sensors check the density of each culture, pH monitors ensure the water stays within fractions of a point of perfection, temperature controls maintain the warmth that tropical strains prefer while cooling the cold-adapted organisms in their separate chambers.

Automated harvesting drones drift along rails above the bioreactors, their soft brushes skimming mature algae from the surfaces and channeling them into processing channels where they become food supplements, pharmaceutical compounds, or simply more inoculum for fresh tanks. The nutrient circulation pumps never stop, delivering calcium and nitrogen and phosphorus in precise ratios, trace elements in quantities so small they would be invisible if not for the sensors that track every drop. At the central monitoring alcove, a curved display shows oxygen output metrics for the entire Pearl—current production rates, projections for the next seventy-two hours, alerts for any deviation from the narrow parameters that keep the colony breathing.

The colonists call this room the lung. They come here to remember what matters.

Children visit on school trips, pressing their faces against the glass and watching the currents swirl within the bioreactors, learning the names of the organisms that make their air possible. Refugees from the lower decks sit on the observation benches that ring the uppermost level, where the light pools softest and the hum of the equipment becomes a kind of white noise meditation. They watch the algae move, these ancient organisms that have been doing the same thing for a billion years, and they remember to breathe deeply. Pregnant women come to feel the rhythms of the room, finding comfort in its constant, reliable life. Old engineers sit here in the blue-green night glow, remembering that every breath they take is a gift from something smaller than they can see, something they have learned to tend.

The room runs itself, mostly. The automation is sophisticated, redundant, capable of maintaining the oxygen supply through disasters that have never yet materialized. But the humans still come, because Syreplantage is where Ombra Prime confronts its deepest truth: that survival here is not mechanical but biological, not industrial but agricultural, not a matter of engineering but of farming. The colonists are gardeners, and the garden is all that keeps them alive.

But not everything in the lung works as intended.

There are secrets hidden in the tanks.

In the furthest bioreactor, the one that maintenance logs show is dedicated to a failed experimental strain, the algae do not behave as they should. They glow brighter than the others at night—much brighter, a phosphorescent white that pulses in patterns too regular to be natural. The xenobiology team has been investigating, and their reports remain classified, filed under the heading of "environmental anomaly, low priority." But the workers who service the harvesting drones have noticed something else: the air near that particular tank tastes different, almost electric, and those who spend too long near it report vivid dreams, heightened senses, a feeling of being watched by something patient and aware.

Some say the engineered extremophiles have crossed with something they should not have. Others whisper that the bioluminescence was always more than a side effect, that the designers of Syreplantage built in more than they admitted. A faction within the colony has begun meeting in the observation benches at night, speaking in low voices about the air we breathe, about who controls it, about what would happen if the forty-seventh tank went dark. They do not call themselves revolutionaries. They call themselves the Oxygenari, and they believe that whoever owns the breath owns the ship.

There is also a data discrepancy in the nutrient logs that an analyst named Maeve Solis has been quietly tracking for three months. The trace element concentrations being consumed by the algae do not match the quantities being supplied. Something is consuming the excess—or someone is removing it. The missing minerals are not valuable enough to steal, but they are exactly the right compounds to synthesize certain organic compounds that are, by law, restricted to medical manufacturing bays. Someone has been using the oxygen farm as a front for something, and the trail leads to the lower levels where the colony's shadow economy thrives.
