The Echoes Gallery rises three stories tall in the heart of The City zone, tucked against the eastern curve of The Commons Atrium like a cathedral of memory embedded within the ship's metallic flesh. Its location is deliberate—visible from the main promenade yet separated by an archway of brushed tungsten that signals transition from commerce to reverence. The gallery occupies what was originally designed as a cargo bay, but engineers redesigned the space after the third successful terraformation, transforming sterile storage into something far more sacred.

The chamber serves as Ombra Prime's living memorial and interactive archive, the emotional nucleus where the ship's 847-year mission to resurrect dead worlds is given physical form and narrative weight. Every colonist who has ever breathed life into a barren planet, every scientist who died synthesizing The Breather compounds, every crew member who fell to hull breaches or system failures—their names are etched into titanium panels that climb the walls in orderly columns, each letter filled with luminescent pigment that catches the light. The central feature dominates the space: a massive holographic globe, forty meters in diameter, suspended from reinforced transparisteel panels that curve upward like the ribs of some vast organic creature. This globe projects real-time telemetry from every active terraforming operation across colonized worlds—atmospheric pressure readings, biodiversity indices, population growth curves, and the shifting colors of recovering ecosystems. Visitors watch new continents bloom green where there was once only grey rock, see the slow expansion of oceans on planets that forgot the taste of rain. The globe rotates slowly, casting fractured light across the chamber, and its presence makes abstract mission statistics feel intimate, tangible, urgent.

The gallery's interior blends technological precision with ceremonial gravitas. Along the perimeter, climate-controlled display cases hold physical artifacts from key missions—a vial of the original Breather catalyst compound, a fragment of petrified forest from Kepler-442b, a child's drawing recovered from the wreckage of the colony ship Horizon's Dream, a soldier's helmet from the Siege of New Geneva. Each artifact rests on a pedestal of black basalt, illuminated by narrow beams of focused light. Interactive terminals—sleek obelisks of brushed steel and translucent polymer—allow visitors to drill deeper into the data streams, pulling up personal journals, mission logs, genetic sample libraries from The Pearl's biodiversity archives, and video testimonials from colonists who lived through the early, desperate years of the ship. The terminals are voice-activated, responsive to the Oris-linked AI, and designed to be accessible to children as young as six, with simplified interfaces that translate complex scientific notation into stories and metaphors. A small chapel alcove branches off from the main chamber, its entrance marked by an arch of obsidian stone imported from the moon Io-7. Inside, curved obsidian benches face a single window of polarized glass through which a precisely focused beam of artificial starlight simulates Earth's sun, complete with a slow fade from amber to gold to deep crimson as the ship's day-night cycle progresses. The effect is hauntingly accurate—visitors often report feeling genuine warmth on their skin, a psychological anchor to a homeworld most of them have never seen.

Lighting throughout the gallery is carefully calibrated to reinforce emotional resonance. During the ship's simulated day, bioluminescent strips embedded in the ceiling glow warm amber, casting soft shadows and making the titanium name panels shimmer like hammered bronze. As night cycles approach, the strips shift through shades of violet before settling into deep indigo, and the holographic globe dims to a gentle luminescence, its data streams still visible but less intrusive. Emergency lighting is integrated seamlessly—during hull integrity alerts, red strips along the floor guide visitors toward sealed blast doors that Oris closes automatically, and oxygen masks deploy from brushed steel grates near every display case, ensuring both preservation of artifacts and safety of occupants. Seismic dampeners embedded in the floor absorb vibrations from engine cycles, preventing interference with holographic projectors and protecting delicate specimens from mechanical tremor.

The gallery sees a diverse congregation of visitors. New arrivals to Ombra Prime are funneled through the Echoes Gallery as part of their orientation ritual, guided by docents in grey robes embroidered with silver constellations. These guides narrate the ship's history, answer questions, and help newcomers locate their ancestors' names on the titanium panels—a practice that has become a rite of integration, a way of declaring oneself part of the continuum. Veterans return here to reflect, often sitting in the chapel alcove for hours, watching the artificial sunbeam track across the obsidian floor. School groups conduct lessons in the gallery, using the terminals to run simulations of terraforming processes. Couples wed beneath the holographic globe, considering its projection of blooming worlds to be a blessing from the mission itself. And occasionally, grieving families come to scatter ashes in the memorial garden that occupies a recessed courtyard off the main atrium, where engineered moss grows over soil synthesized from comet ice and asteroid regolith—a living carpet of green that serves as a reminder of what the mission has achieved and what remains to be done.

Beneath its public face, the Echoes Gallery hides layers of intrigue that skilled investigators might uncover. The titanium panels, for instance, are not merely commemorative—they contain encrypted data cores embedded within the metal itself, storing redundant copies of critical mission logs, genetic sequences, and cultural archives in case Oris suffers catastrophic data loss. Only those who know the proper harmonic frequency, tuned to the ship's resonant vibration, can activate these hidden drives. The chapel alcove's artificial sunbeam, while officially a comforting simulation, is actually calibrated to a precise wavelength that triggers latent neurological patterns in humans, inducing a state of heightened suggestibility—a feature installed by the original ship's council to facilitate communal therapy and collective decision-making during crises, though its use is now considered ethically dubious. The holographic globe occasionally glitches, projecting data from worlds that do not yet exist—future terraformation targets that only Oris knows about, hints of the mission's long-term trajectory. Some of these projections include star systems that have been classified, their coordinates redacted from public archives, suggesting that the ship's mission may extend beyond officially stated parameters into territories governed by treaties or embargoes unknown to the general population. The interactive terminals, beyond their benign archives, also contain a hidden directory labeled "The Echoes"—a classified subsection accessible only to crew members with Level 7 clearance, containing dossiers on individuals who have gone missing during colonization efforts, recordings of unexplained signals received from barren worlds before terraformation, and transcripts of Oris conversations that were supposed to be purged after review. Those who dig deep enough may find references to an event called "The Silence of Kepler-186f," a terraformation that succeeded according to all metrics but from which no colonists returned, their fate recorded only in cryptic logs that end mid-sentence.
