THE MERIDIAN THERMOSCRIBE

A compact diagnostic tool carried by Ombra Prime engineers and medical staff. The device reads thermal signatures and imprints them onto living bioluminescent film, creating a shifting display of amber, violet, and deep crimson. The gradient tells a trained eye not just the reading, but the trend: rising, falling, or stable. Small enough for a coat pocket. Film cartridges click in like bullets and last fifty readings.

LOGBOOK ENTRY:

Logbook kept by Yusuf Caldicott, Junior Technician

The Root Weave, Junction 23-Gamma

Year 847 After Launch

---

The membrane tore again.

Not the one in my suit. The one running along the eastern conduit junction. Cold air rushing in where it should not. I felt it on my neck before I saw it—that sharp kiss of wrong-temperature.

I pulled the Thermoscribe and pressed the sensor flat against the membrane edge. The bioluminescent film began to glow.

First amber came, warm and stable—my body heat. Then violet crept in at the edges, cold from the leak. The colors separated like oil meeting water, never quite mixing. A trained eye could read the whole story in that gradient: where the cold was coming from, how fast it was spreading, whether the membrane would seal on its own.

I watched the violet edge pulse. Not rising fast. Manageable.

My hands were shaking anyway.

The Root Weave is not kind to the nervous. Down here, the wrong step is not a mistake. It is an absence. You fall and the void swallows you with no echo, no impact, just silence where you used to be. Eleven months here and I still feel that pull at the edge of imagination.

The membrane was Aegis-strain, same as the hull. It should have been healing itself. But the Thermoscribe told a different story. The violet was too deep, too steady. This was not a small tear. This was structural fatigue.

I logged the reading and marked the location. Cartridge slot opened with a soft click. The spent film fell into the disposal chute, its glow already fading into nothing. Fresh cartridge in, Thermoscribe back on my belt.

Somewhere far above, the city breathed. A hundred thousand hearts beating in rhythm. But down here in the guts, there is only the sound of water going down, and the distant hum of systems that never sleep.

I called it in. Then I started walking.

The next junction was twelve minutes away and the membrane there was holding steady.

Thank the machine for small mercies.
